Education

I graduated Beloit College majoring in economics and minoring in physics. I have been interested in financial markets since I was a kid. As a middle schooler, I had an Etrade account where I threw around a whole hundred bucks. That's where I learned the harsh realities of service fees. In high school I was the president of my school's stock club, planning weekly meetings and organizing annual trips to visit the Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago This all meant that coming into college I knew I wanted to study markets. I just had no clue where to begin.

I started a few months after Andrej Karpathy's The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Recurrent Neural Networks was published. At the time, I didn't understand matrix algebra, let alone complicated loss functions. That didn't stop this article, and the papers that followed it, from dominating many of my academic discussions at the time. This made me interested in how these methods would be applied to finance. I shared my curiousity with some of my teachers - Dr. Munro, professor of data analytics; Dr. Moulton, professor of econometrics - who encouraged me to take programming more seriously. It was under Dr. Moulton where I was awarded a research grant to study financial forecasting through the Sanger Research Program.

During the Sanger Research, programming changed from being a tool I used, to one of my main hobbies. I spent my senior year formalizing my understanding of concepts related to programming and computer science. that is a short explanation for how I went from economics to software engineering.
